# Chapter 4

Hainan Province
MSS Signals Intelligence Facility 
03:47 local time

Seventeen minutes.

Li Wenhao marked it silently, the way he always did - not writing it down, not even fully thinking it, just letting the number settle into the running tally he kept in some back room of his mind. Seventeen minutes since the last system flag. Longer than average for this hour. He'd predicted twelve.
The game meant nothing. He knew that. A private ritual, no more significant than the way some people tap doorframes or count stairs. But it gave the nights form, imposed a structure his attention could lean against. And occasionally - rarely - the system behaved exactly as he expected, and he felt a small, irrational satisfaction. Order, briefly proven.

He stretched in his chair, feeling his spine complain. Twenty-nine years old and already he sat like someone older, hunched toward the screen, shoulders curling inward. His mother had warned him about this. You'll ruin your posture, staring at that computer all day. She still thought he worked in network administration for a telecom company. A version of the truth with all the meaning removed.
The operations floor was quiet in the way that 3 a.m. was always quiet - not silent, but muffled, as though sound itself had thickened. The hum of the air conditioning, set too cold as always, undercut by the softer breath of server racks in the adjacent room. Keyboards clicking in sporadic bursts. Someone's chair creaking as they shifted weight. Twelve workstations, four occupied. The skeleton hours, when the algorithms processed and humans watched.

Li reached for his coffee and found it cold.

He drank it anyway. Nescafé Gold Blend, the imported kind he bought online in bulk because it was the only instant that didn't leave a metallic film on his tongue. Even cold, it was better than the communal jar in the kitchenette - that stuff tasted like burnt rubber, marketed as arabica. Small preferences, privately maintained. Strategies for surviving a job that offered no other form of self-expression.

Three rows down, Chen Rui cracked his knuckles. The sound was soft but distinct, a sequential popping that preceded every escalation decision, every flagged file, every moment of professional anxiety. Li had learned to read those knuckles like weather. Frequent cracking meant Chen Rui was wound tight, seeing threats in everything, ready to wake supervisors over keyword coincidences. Silence meant he'd settled into something, focus narrowing, the nervous energy temporarily channelled.

Right now: silence. Chen Rui was reading something closely, energy drink forgotten beside his keyboard, the silver can sweating onto a coaster he'd fashioned from a piece of torn cardboard.

Li turned back to his own screen. The queue had refreshed: forty-one items, down from forty-seven when his shift began. Processed, filed, forgotten. The metabolism of signals intelligence, in which vast quantities of data were ingested and almost none of it mattered.

He opened the next item. Shipping manifest from a Shenzhen logistics company, flagged for a destination address that matched a research facility on a watchlist. He scanned the contents - industrial parts, laboratory glassware, nothing remarkable - checked the end-user certification, noted that the flag had been triggered by address proximity rather than content. Cleared it. Nineteen seconds.

Next item. Academic conference registration, an American professor of materials science planning to attend a symposium in Wuhan. The algorithm had flagged a co-author on one of his papers, three degrees of separation from someone who'd once consulted for a defence contractor. Li pulled the publication record, found nothing but carbon nanotube research with civilian applications. Cleared it - forty one seconds.

He worked through six more items without pause, decisions flowing through him with the automated consistency of long practice. This was what the job actually was: not analysis, not investigation, but triage. Looking where the system pointed and deciding whether the pointing was justified. A human checkpoint in a process that was slowly, quietly, questioning whether it needed human checkpoints at all.

His phone was in the locker - it always was, during shifts - but he thought of it anyway. Thought of checking for a message from Lin Xiaoyu, though he knew there wouldn't be one. She'd be asleep by now, had been asleep for hours, the apartment dark except for the extractor hood light she always left on when he worked nights. A small courtesy. He'd stopped mentioning it because mentioning it might make her stop.

They rarely talked anymore, not really. They exchanged logistics. Rice in fridge. I'll be late. Don't forget rent transfer. Notes left on the kitchen counter, texts sending schedules that never quite aligned. Two years together and they'd somehow become roommates who shared a bed on alternating half-days, bodies occupying the same space without overlapping.
It hadn't always been this way. Or maybe it had, and he was only now noticing. Hard to tell. Hard to know whether the distance was new or simply newly visible.

He remembered - surfacing uninvited, the way memories did during long shifts - coming home last week to find her laughing at her phone. Softly, privately, her back to the bedroom door. When he'd asked what was funny, she'd said nothing important without looking up. The laughter wasn't meant for him. He understood that. What he didn't understand was when her laughter had stopped being something she shared.

The air conditioning cycled, and Li pulled his jacket tighter. Everyone who worked nights kept layers nearby. The system was calibrated for machines, not bodies - the servers in the adjacent room needed cool air more than the humans processing their output. You adapted. You stopped complaining. You draped a jacket over your chair and forgot that the first week you'd found it unbearable.

Outside, he knew, the Hainan night was warm and thick. January here meant nothing like January in Beijing or Shanghai - no grey chill, no breath misting in the air. Just the same subtropical weight, the humidity that clung to skin the moment you stepped out of climate control. He'd arrived four hours ago with his shirt already damp from the walk between bus stop and facility, the security checkpoint's air conditioning hitting him like a wall of cold water.

He didn't mind the heat, not really. It was honest. What he minded was the way Hainan felt like nowhere - not punishment, not opportunity, just a posting. A place where data arrived first and meaning was added elsewhere. He sat at the mouth of a river and never saw where it flowed.

Temporary, he told himself. Everyone did. The first year, you believed it. The second year, you suspected it might not be true. By the third year - 

He wasn't at three years yet. Still time.

The queue refreshed again. Forty items now. Chen Rui had cleared one, the soft click of his mouse audible in the room's hush, the exhalation that followed suggesting it had been a close call, something he'd nearly escalated before thinking better of it. Or maybe something he'd escalated after all. Hard to tell from here.

Li put in his left earbud - just the one, volume low - and resumed the podcast he'd been half-listening to before the tally distracted him. A calm voice discussing the history of cryptography, the evolution from ancient ciphers to modern encryption. The interesting thing about the Enigma machine, the voice was saying, is that the code itself wasn't the vulnerability. It was the human procedures around it - the assumptions, the shortcuts, the patterns that operators fell into without realising. Content that filled space without demanding attention. Background texture for a mind that needed occupation while it waited.

He opened the next item in the queue.
And stopped.

The flag wasn't unusual - medium priority, 0.57 confidence score, source tagged as Key-logger intercept from a maritime vessel in the central Pacific. The system had clustered it under "动能武器/弹道" - Kinetic Weaponry/Ballistics - which covered everything from actual missile tests to physics research papers that happened to trigger the wrong keywords.
What made him pause was the machine translation.

The intercepted communication had already been processed by the automated system, the English stripped away and replaced with rough Chinese that Li could actually read. But something about the rendering felt wrong. The phrases sat awkwardly on the screen, highlighted by the keyword triggers that had routed the file here:
...屏障穿透行为，我们无法解释... (barrier penetration behaviour we cannot explain)
...载荷密度超出图表... (payload density exceeds chart)
...相干持续性在任何温度下都不应该可能... (coherence persistence should not be possible at any temperature)

Li read it twice. Then a third time.

His English was rudimentary at best - enough to recognise brand names, to parse simple emails, to know when a translation had gone sideways. The system provided the original text in a collapsible field, and he expanded it now, staring at the English words without fully comprehending them. Barrier penetration. Payload density. Coherence persistence. The terms looked technical. Scientific, maybe? But the machine had rendered them into Chinese that carried a military weight - 穿透 for penetration, 载荷 for payload - words that belonged to weapons specifications, not research abstracts.

His pattern recognition - the thing he was good at, the skill that had earned him solid-but-unexceptional test scores and a posting in signals intake - was firing in a way it rarely did. The phrases looked like weapons language. But they didn't feel like weapons language - not quite. There was something underneath the military terminology, something the machine had paved over in its rush to categorise. He couldn't name it. He could only sense its absence, the way you sense a word on the tip of your tongue.

The file had been tagged as weapons-related, and that tag could shape everything downstream. Translators would work from the military frame. Analysts would look for threat indicators. The original meaning - whatever it was - would bend toward the interpretation the system had already imposed.
He could clear it. Mark it as a false positive, a civilian research communication miscategorised by keyword coincidence. It would vanish into the statistical noise, one of thousands of items processed and forgotten every night.

Or he could escalate.

Escalation meant the file would move up the chain, acquire context he didn't have, be evaluated by people with wider views and more authority. If it mattered, they would catch it. If it didn't, it would dissolve into committees and be forgotten anyway. Either way, the responsibility would no longer be his.
Li thought about Chen Rui, two rows down, who escalated too often and was tolerated for it because over-reporting was safer than silence. He thought about the supervisors who could be woken, the additional work added to someone else's queue, the small professional cost of being the technician who flagged things that turned out to be nothing.
He thought about his fear - the one he never examined directly - that his decisions didn't matter at all. That escalation was theatre. That outcomes were driven by forces so large his participation was cosmetic.

And he thought, briefly, about the opposite possibility: that this one mattered, and he was the only person who would ever look at it closely, and the choice he made in the next thirty seconds would ripple outward in ways he couldn't see.

The thought was uncomfortable. He set it aside.

His cursor hovered over the escalate button. The file would climb the chain, gather interpretations, become someone else's responsibility. Or it would dissolve, one more false positive in a system built to generate them. Either way, he would never know. That was the architecture: responsibility transferred without feedback, decisions made without consequences - or with consequences so distant, so diffused, that they might as well not exist.

He clicked.

He added a brief note: Machine translation may not understand technical context. Recommend specialist review of terminology before routing. Watched the file leave his queue.
The relief was immediate. Not pride - he hadn't done anything to be proud of - but the small unburdening that came whenever responsibility transferred. The file was someone else's problem now. Someone with more context. Better judgment. A wider view.

He refreshed the queue. Thirty-nine items.

The podcast murmured in his ear, something about the Enigma machine, the mathematical foundations of code-breaking. He let it wash over him without listening. The vents exhaled their steady chill. Chen Rui shifted in his chair, reached for his energy drink, set it down again without drinking.

Li reached for his mug, remembered it was empty, and walked to the kitchenette to make more coffee. The kettle's noise was the loudest sound on the floor. He waited, watching steam rise, thinking about nothing in particular.

When he returned to his station, he patted the empty pocket where his phone had been out of habit - instinct persisting even though he knew the phone was in a locker two corridors away. No way to know if Lin Xiaoyu had texted. No reason to think she would have. She'd be asleep, the apartment dark, the light burning for no one.

He sat down. Adjusted his jacket. Opened the next item in the queue.

Thirty-eight.

The night continued, patient and featureless, offering no indication that anything had changed. Li worked through the hours the way he always did - methodical, careful, routine - and by the time his shift ended, he had forgotten the file entirely.

It was already in Guangzhou by then. By morning, it would reach Beijing.

But Li didn't know that. He walked out into the warm Hainan dawn, felt the humidity settle on his skin like a second shirt, and thought about whether Lin Xiaoyu would still be there when he got home, and whether he wanted her to be, and what he would say if she was.

The bus came. He boarded. The city scrolled past, already bright, already loud, indifferent to the thing he had set in motion.

He checked his phone. No messages.
He put it away, leaned his head against the window, and closed his eyes.
