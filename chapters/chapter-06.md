# Chapter 6: Calculation

Quantiv Communications Headquarters
Bethesda, Maryland 09:14 local time

The morning sun hit her screen at exactly the angle that made the numbers illegible.

Rachel stood, crossed to the window, and lowered the blind until the sunlight slid off her screen. The room dimmed a fraction - enough. Outside, Maryland went about its morning: coats pulled tight, heads down, traffic moving steadily through the cold. She watched for a moment, then returned to her desk.

The depreciation spreadsheet was waiting where she’d left it.
Her morning had a structure - antioxidant tea at 7:30, inbox triage until 8:15, the executive briefing at 8:30 that Julian invariably cut short, then the administrative work that accumulated like residue in the gaps between meetings. The schedules were part of that residue: unglamorous, vital, the kind of task that reminded her how much of leadership was simply maintenance.

The unopened message had been sitting in her inbox for four days.

She had noticed it arrive - Marcus Holt, PhD, Marine Geochemistry, Co-Chief Scientist, JOIDES Resolution - and left it where it was. The name, the academic affiliation: all familiar enough to categorise. She saw messages like this regularly. Most resolved themselves by being ignored.
This one had not.

At 9:47, having worked through the depreciation schedules and felt her concentration begin to thin, Rachel allowed herself to click.

The message was three paragraphs long, carefully constructed. The studied informality of someone who had drafted and redrafted before sending.

Rachel - hope this finds you well. It’s been too long since that AGU session in San Francisco. I’ve been thinking about some of the work your team presented, and I wonder if you might have a few minutes to discuss something we’ve encountered out here…

She read it twice. Then a third time, more slowly.

Strange material coming up in deep-sea cores… density values that exceed anything in the literature… thermal properties that remind me of the coherence challenges you were wrestling with back in Maryland…
This was not a funding request.

Rachel sat back in her chair. The depreciation schedules glowing on her screen, suddenly irrelevant. Four days. She had let this sit for four days because she’d assumed she already understood what it was.

The language was hedged, appropriately cautious. But beneath it she could hear something else - the particular frequency of someone holding something extraordinary and trying very hard to sound calmer than he felt.

She had learned to recognise that frequency.
It preceded most of the disappointments.
 She could forward it to the Quantiv materials team. Get a preliminary read on whether the terminology held water. That was the sensible thing - the collaborative thing. The thing that distributed risk.

She did not forward it.

She had met him four years earlier at a conference reception - adequate wine, forgettable conversation. He’d approached her near the keynote session, holding his glass with both hands. Early forties, she’d guessed. The kind of academic who had done solid work without ever quite breaking through to the first rank.

They’d talked for perhaps twenty minutes. He was knowledgeable about the challenges facing cutting-edge quantum research — surprisingly so, for someone whose expertise was geochemistry. He asked good questions. He listened with an attention that felt hungry.
She had accepted his LinkedIn request afterward. Sent a brief follow-up. Professional warmth, precisely measured.
He had replied within the hour.

She had taken three days.
The asymmetry had been clear even then.

The executive floor at Quantiv occupied the top level of a glass-fronted building Julian had selected personally, over Nadia’s objections about lease terms and parking ratios. He’d wanted the light, he said. The sense of elevation. Rachel understood what he actually wanted: the visual grammar of arrival.

She walked the corridor now, heels muted on pale carpet, the message from Marcus Holt printed and folded in her portfolio. She could have forwarded it electronically - Julian preferred digital - but physical documents changed the temperature of a conversation. They implied judgement. Selection.
Julian’s office occupied the corner. Floor-to-ceiling windows on two sides, the Bethesda skyline visible in one direction and the green sprawl of the naval medical centre in the other. The door was open.

He was on the phone when she entered, standing at the window, posture signalling that the call was nearly over. She waited.

“We’ll revisit after the quarter,” he said. “The fundamentals haven’t changed — leave the board to me.” A pause. “Right. Talk soon.”
He turned.

“Rachel. What’s this?”
“Something interesting,” she said. “Potentially. From an oceanographic contact.”

She handed him the printout. Watched him skim it - the rapid pattern-matching that passed for reading. She had highlighted the relevant passages. He wouldn’t read the rest.

"Room-temperature quantum coherence," he said. The words landed differently from the rest - not skimmed, but weighed. "He's claiming that?"
"He's speculating. But something made him reach for those words." She gestured toward the printout. "It's all there — thermal anomalies, density figures. I don't have the background to evaluate it. That's why I brought it to you."
Julian looked back at the page. "From deep-sea mud."

"Deep-sea sediment." She paused. "He thinks he's found something new — and he doesn't have the resources to prove it.” 
Julian’s eyes lifted.
“And he’s reaching out to us because…?”
“Because we presented on quantum coherence at AGU. Because he doesn’t have the equipment to characterise what he’s found.”
Julian looked back at the page, then set it down.
“This is thin.”
“I know.”
“Speculation. No samples. No independent verification.” He gestured once. “I don’t have bandwidth for interesting maybes. I need something I can use.”
 Rachel nodded.

"If your contact has something concrete - samples, data, reproducible results - then we can talk. But I'm not going to redirect attention based on -“ He gestured at the printout, still lying on his desk like something discarded. "Based on a hopeful note from someone looking for validation."  She absorbed the rebuke without reaction, collected the printout from his desk - he hadn't offered to return it, which meant he didn't consider it worth keeping - and walked back toward the door.
 “I’ll follow up,” she said, and left.

Back at her desk, Rachel sat without moving.
The depreciation schedules were still open. Her tea had gone cold. Through the glass partition she could see her team working — steady, absorbed, confident that their effort accumulated toward something meaningful.

Julian’s dismissal replayed in her mind - not the refusal itself, but its shape.
Interesting maybes. Validation.

He’d reduced Marcus Holt’s message to psychology.
She opened the email again and read it differently. Not for the claims, but for the tone beneath them. The eagerness threaded through the professionalism. The care with which Marcus positioned himself as offering something without quite daring to name it.

He wants to be seen, she thought. She had seen that hunger before. It was common enough. It was also useful. Rachel opened a new window and began to type.

She drafted the reply three times.
The first was too warm.
The second too distant.
The third held.

Marcus — wonderful to hear from you. What you’re describing sounds genuinely intriguing, and I appreciate you thinking of Quantiv.

I should be transparent with you: bringing preliminary findings to our leadership is challenging without something concrete to anchor the conversation. There’s a lot of noise in this space, and distinguishing signal requires evidence that can be independently assessed.

She paused.

That said, I find your observations compelling enough to want to learn more. If there’s any possibility of obtaining samples — even a small quantity, for preliminary characterisation — it would transform this from an interesting hypothesis into something I could advocate for internally.
No pressure. I know the constraints of shipboard work. But if an opportunity arises, I wanted you to know there’s genuine interest here.

She re-read it.
Then she added one final line.

Quantiv has a history of recognising the people who help us identify breakthrough opportunities. We don’t forget those contributions.

She hovered for a moment.
Then she clicked send.

The message vanished into the system. Somewhere over the Pacific, it would find a satellite window and descend to the Resolution. Marcus would read it tonight, or tomorrow, or whenever shipboard rhythms allowed. He would understand the invitation. He would make his choice.

Rachel closed the email and returned to her depreciation schedules. The numbers resolved themselves easily now - her mind had cleared, the decision made, the weight transferred. She worked steadily through the afternoon, leaving comments on a draft press release, reviewing projections, executing the administrative tasks that would leave no trace of what had actually occupied her attention.

I’m not forcing anyone, she told herself. I’m just opening a door.

The thought felt true.
It would feel less true later.

At 6:40, she logged out and gathered her things. The office had emptied around her - the usual Friday exodus, people peeling away toward weekends and families and lives that existed outside these glass walls. Rachel moved through the quiet floor alone, her heels marking a steady rhythm.

The elevator arrived empty. She stepped in and pressed the button for the parking garage, watching the floor numbers descend: 7, 6, 5. Her reflection floated in the polished doors - composed, professional, giving nothing away.

In her bag was the notebook she would open tonight at the hotel. The same suite she always booked when she worked late. Where she would write down the names of everyone involved in this thread, just in case.

The elevator reached the garage. The doors opened onto concrete and silence and the distant hum of ventilation. Rachel walked to her car, unlocked it, slid into the driver’s seat.

She did not start the engine.
She sat in the dark, hands resting on the wheel, running through the implications one more time. Marcus would respond or he wouldn’t. He would take the risk or he wouldn’t. She had given him the opening; the choice was his. That was how these things worked. That was how you kept your hands clean.

The garage was empty. The concrete pillars stood in rows, receding into shadow. Somewhere above her, the building she’d just left was settling into night - monitors fading, lights dimming, the small green power indicators holding their patient watch over empty desks.

Rachel started the car.

The engine turned over. The headlights carved a path through the dark. She pulled out of the space and drove toward the exit, toward the hotel, toward the notebook waiting to record what she had set in motion.

Behind her, the garage returned to stillness.

And three miles above the Pacific, moving at the speed of light, a carefully crafted message continued its journey toward a man who had already decided to say yes.
