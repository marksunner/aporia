# Chapter 10: Marionettist

The Ritz-Carlton Chevy Chase
Bethesda, Maryland 
05:47 local time

Rachel woke before the alarm, as she always did in hotels. The unfamiliarity kept her light - strange sheets, strange air, the distinctive silence of a room that belonged to no one. She lay still for a moment, letting the day come into focus before anything tried to claim it.
Grey light gathered at the curtains, the city beyond them already awake and in motion.
Her phone remained face-down on the desk, exactly where she'd placed it the night before, screen against wood, notifications silenced. This was how Rachel managed information: she controlled when she knew things.
She swung her legs out of bed - the industrial carpet too anonymous to feel like anything.
In the bathroom, she studied her face in the mirror - not for age or fatigue, but for tells. The slight tension around the eyes that surfaced when she was thinking too hard. The set of her mouth when she was about to say something she hadn't quite decided to say. The upkeep of composure. She adjusted the light a fraction warmer and brushed her teeth without looking at herself again.
She made tea, her preferred antioxidant blend a permanent staple in her travel bag, handling the sachet with care she would never extend to the sad items in the hospitality tray. The ritual performed but not inhabited. Her mind was already elsewhere, running scenarios, rehearsing phrases she might need later.
She took her vitamins—the same ones, in the same order, with the same glass of bottled water. Wednesday's routine, indistinguishable from Tuesday's, from any day's.
Only then did she allow herself to check.
The phone's screen lit at her touch. She saw his name immediately: Marcus Holt. A timestamp of 2:38 AM - though the ship ran hours behind the East Coast, so he'd have sent it mid-evening his time. The preview text visible enough to tell her he'd come through. She did not open it.
Set the phone back down. Face-down.
She finished her tea. Retrieved her notebook from the nightstand - the slim Moleskine she carried everywhere, the one where she wrote down names and half-formed thoughts and questions she didn't want to forget. She didn't write anything, not yet. Just held it.
The discipline was important. The delay was the point. Anyone could be reactive; Rachel had built a career on the pause before response, the controlled interval that let her act rather than react.
She showered, dressed, applied makeup with the efficiency of someone who had reduced the process to its bare essentials. Professional armour, nothing more. By 6:40 she was ready. By 6:45 she was sitting at the small hotel room desk, notebook open, phone still face-down beside her.
She reached for the phone.
The message was brief. Carefully constructed - she could see the drafts behind it, the sentences tried and discarded. The studied informality of someone trying very hard to sound calmer than he felt.
Rachel
Following up on our earlier exchange.
I've secured a small physical sample from the core material I mentioned. grams, not kilograms, but sufficient for preliminary characterisation if handled carefully.
To be clear: this is part of routine shipboard analysis, fully documented at source. No special accommodation required.
If there's still interest on your end, I'd welcome guidance on next steps.
Best, Marcus
Not because she didn't understand — she understood completely — but because she was already thinking past it.
To be clear. The phrase was a tell. No special accommodation required. He was building a defence he didn't quite know he was building. A legal and moral architecture constructed in advance of the need. The sample existed. Marcus had crossed a line he was pretending he hadn't crossed.
She filed the observation under useful, not troubling.
And Julian, who had dismissed the first message as "interesting maybes” - who had left her printout on his desk like something discarded - now had something concrete.
The day had a different weight now.
She opened the notebook. Found a fresh page. Wrote: the date. Marcus Holt. Sample secured. Next: J.
She closed the notebook, slipped it into her bag, and went down to check out.

The drive to Quantiv took forty-five minutes. Rachel knew the route well enough to navigate without thinking, which was useful — she wasn't thinking about the road.
She was thinking about deployment.
Julian had dismissed Marcus four days ago. Interesting maybes. Someone looking for validation. If she walked in now with even a trace of vindication, his ego would snag before his mind engaged. That was not how you moved Julian. She needed to present the problem, not the win. Let him arrive at the significance himself.
Traffic slowed at the Wisconsin Avenue interchange. Her hands rested loose on the wheel, her mind working angles.
Marcus was on a ship in the Pacific. The sample was with him. Quantiv needed both. How do you extract a scientist from a research vessel without raising flags?
You give him a reason to leave. A reason that feels like recognition.
The Global Satellite Communications Summit — San Francisco, late February. Quantiv was a silver sponsor. The conference had a quantum encryption track: cryptographers, communications engineers. Certainly not oceanographers.
Marcus presenting there would be a stretch. Rachel knew this. She also knew Marcus wouldn't see it. What he would see: a prestigious conference, a speaking invitation, Quantiv's interest in his work. The flattery would be so loud he wouldn't notice the venue didn't fit. He'd been overlooked for so long that any recognition would feel like vindication.
Somewhere in the planning, a thought surfaced: This is Potemkin. You're constructing recognition out of nothing.
She didn't dwell on it. The conference was real. The exposure was real. And if the material turned out to be what he claimed, everyone benefits.
The Quantiv building appeared on the horizon, glass catching the morning light.
By the time she pulled into the garage, she'd made her decision: present the problem, not the solution. Let Julian's mind work. If the conference idea needed to surface, she could offer it as practical logistics to a problem he was already engaged with.
She was the architect. She didn't need the credit. She wanted the result.
She turned off the engine. Checked her face in the mirror - composed, professional, giving nothing away. Gathered her things.

Rachel walked the corridor to Julian's office. Same pale carpet, same visual grammar of arrival - but her posture was different. She wasn't bringing interesting maybes. She was bringing proof of concept.
The printout was in her portfolio again. Marcus's email, formatted clean. The physical document calibrated to change the gravity of the conversation.
As she approached Julian's door, it opened. Dr. Sergei Volkov—Quantiv's chief scientist—exited, a laptop under one arm, expression neutral. They exchanged a nod, professional, brief. Two people who knew each other's position in the hierarchy. Whatever conversation had just ended stayed behind the door.
Rachel registered this without breaking stride. Julian's world was populated by important people seeking his attention. She had access, but so did others. She wasn't uniquely privileged—just prepared.
The previous conversation still hung in the air. She let it clear.
Julian was at his desk, not on the phone, not signalling impatience. Floor-to-ceiling windows caught the winter light. He was reviewing something on his screen but looked up when she entered.
"Rachel."
She didn't sit. Not yet. This conversation would be short or long depending on how the first thirty seconds landed.
"Marcus Holt came through."
A small moment of silence. Julian's expression didn't change, but something shifted behind his eyes. He remembered the name, remembered dismissing it.
"The oceanographer."
"He's secured a sample. Small, grams, but sufficient for a preliminary characterisation." She placed the printout on his desk. "Room-temperature coherence. If that's real.."
“It would be transformative." Julian finished the sentence. Not because she'd led him there, because his mind had already leapt ahead. He was reading the email now, properly, not the rapid pattern-matching from before. Actually reading.
Rachel watched his dismissiveness give way to calculation. Calculation give way to intensity. This was the reversal she'd anticipated, but she didn't savour it. She was already thinking three moves ahead.
Julian looked up. "What do we know about the source material?"
"Deep-sea sediment. Thirty-four million years old, according to Marcus. Says it contains a microscopic substance with properties that don't match anything on record."
"And he's... where? Still on the ship?"
“The Resolution. Mid-Pacific. He's running operations on board, as far as I know.”
Julian glanced toward the door - the direction Volkov had gone. "Have you shared this with Sergei?"
"No."
"Good. Keep it that way for now." He set the printout down. "I don't want him chasing shadows. Not until we have something in hand."
Julian stood. Moved to the window. This was how he thought—in motion, looking at horizons. Rachel waited.

"We need that sample in our lab. And we need him available for questions — where it came from, how he obtained it, what the story is if anyone asks downstream." He turned. "How do we make that happen without attracting scrutiny?"
This was the moment she'd prepared for.
"There's an option. The Global Satellite Communications Summit, San Francisco, late February. We're a silver sponsor. There's a quantum encryption track."
Julian frowned slightly. "He's an oceanographer?”
"He wouldn't present as an oceanographer. He'd present on the theoretical challenge, or whatever the proper framing is. Room-temperature effects, potential for new materials. Nothing that discloses the discovery. But enough to justify the invitation."
She watched Julian process. The conference was a stretch, they both knew it. But the function was sound: a legitimate reason for Marcus to leave the ship, travel to San Francisco, hand over the sample in a context that looked like routine academic networking.
"He'd go for that?"
Rachel allowed herself a small, careful observation. "He's been overlooked for a long time. A speaking invitation from a major technology company, quantum materials focus, expenses covered..." She didn't finish the sentence. Didn't need to.
Julian nodded slowly. He was seeing it now, not just the logistics, but the psychology. The extraction dressed as recognition. The manipulation that didn't look like manipulation because it gave Marcus something he genuinely wanted.
"So." Rachel's voice stayed level. "Do I have budget and authority to manufacture an opportunity that removes Marcus from the ship voluntarily?"
The question hung for a moment.
Every word defensible. Every word misdirection. Budget and authority—proper corporate governance. Manufacture an opportunity —event logistics, conference planning. Removes Marcus from the ship—he's choosing to leave; they're facilitating. Voluntarily—the key word, the legal insulation, the lie that wasn't quite a lie.
"Set it up."
That was the authorisation. "Set it up" would eventually mean a helicopter to a research vessel in the Pacific, but the paper trail would show only a conference invitation. A corridor, not an extraction.

If this ever surfaced, in a legal review, an ethics inquiry, a board question, Julian had heard "conference invitation" and approved marketing spend. He hadn't heard "helicopter extraction of a compromised scientist." Rachel had created a paper trail that protected them both.
Or appeared to.
But Julian's mind hadn't stopped. Rachel could see him thinking past the immediate problem, into territory that was still speculative but already felt urgent.
"This sample—grams, you said. Where did it come from? A single source?”
"Apparently one location, yes.”
"Is there more? At the site?"
"I don't know. Marcus might."
Julian turned back to the window. The Bethesda skyline, the pale morning light, the world going about its business unaware of what might be sitting in a vial on a ship in the Pacific.
"If this material is what he says it is—room-temperature coherence, stable, reproducible—grams won't be enough. We'd need... significant quantity. For proper characterisation. For development. For..." He didn't finish. Didn't need to. Manufacturing hung in the air unspoken.
"That's premature," Rachel said. Carefully. Not disagreeing—calibrating.
"Of course it is. But I want to know." He turned back to face her. "When you talk to him—find out what he knows about scale. Recoverability. Whether this is a fluke or a deposit."
Rachel nodded. Filed it away. Another piece of information to transmit, another question to seed in Marcus's mind.
How much is there?
There was a moment here—barely a moment—where Rachel could have paused. Could have asked herself: what happens to Marcus if this works? He would leave the ship carrying stolen material. He would hand it to strangers in a hotel lobby or a conference hallway. He would have committed himself to something he didn't fully understand, for people who only saw him as a delivery mechanism.
The thought surfaced. She noted it. Filed it under not my problem—or perhaps manageable later—and moved on.
"I'll reach out to him today. Frame the invitation, get a sense of his timeline."
"Good." Julian was already turning back to his desk, the conversation filed under handled, his mind moving to the next thing. "Keep me informed. And Rachel—"
She paused at the door.
"Good work. Staying on this."
It wasn't an apology for dismissing her before. Julian didn't apologise. But it was an acknowledgment—the closest he came to admitting he'd been wrong. Rachel accepted it with a small nod, her expression neutral, and left.

Rachel's office was on the same floor, smaller, without the corner views. The building hummed around her—her team working beyond the glass partition, the ordinary rhythm of a Wednesday morning.
She stood at the window, looking out at nothing in particular. Bethesda going about its business. Traffic, pedestrians, the thin winter light that seemed to flatten everything it touched.
The conversation with Julian was already receding—filed, processed, complete. What remained was execution.
She knew what she would write. The scaffolding was already clear.
The invitation—a speaking slot at the Global Satellite Communications Summit, quantum encryption track. Framed as recognition, as opportunity. Your work on coherence phenomena would be a perfect fit for the audience we're convening.
The flattery—calibrated, precise, not excessive. She knew exactly how much warmth Marcus needed. Enough to feel seen. Not so much that he might question it.
The logistics—first-class travel, accommodation, expenses covered. We take care of the people who help us.
And buried in the warmth, almost an afterthought: Julian's question. I know it's early days, but Julian was curious about the broader context—the site, the deposit, whether there might be potential for scale. Just thinking ahead. No pressure.
Each element true. Each element also manipulation. But that word didn't surface in her thinking. What surfaced was: reasonable. Practical. Everyone gets what they want.
She reached into her bag for the Moleskine, found the morning's note. Underneath, she wrote: GSCS — SF — Feb. J authorised. Scale question seeded. A few words, almost shorthand. Enough to reconstruct the thread later, if she needed to.
Not insurance. Good practice. Keeping track of the moving pieces. The kind of documentation that distinguished professionals from amateurs.
She closed the notebook. Returned it to her bag.
Outside, the morning continued. Cars moved through intersections. People walked with purpose toward destinations that mattered to them. The world indifferent to what was being set in motion in this small office, in this unremarkable moment.
Rachel saw her reflection in the glass—faint, translucent, imposed over the cityscape. Composed. Professional. No trace of what had just been decided.
She didn't feel triumph. Didn't feel doubt. What she felt was simpler: the settled clarity of scaffolding completed. A corridor would soon exist between a ship in the Pacific and a conference room in San Francisco. A man she barely knew would walk through it, carrying something he shouldn't have taken, toward a recognition that wasn't quite real.
But that's not my doing, she thought. He made his choice. I'm just... facilitating.
The thought felt so true she almost believed it.
She turned from the window, sat at her desk and opened her laptop.
The cursor blinked in an empty compose window.
She knew exactly what to say. She always did.
