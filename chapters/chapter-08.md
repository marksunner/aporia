# Chapter 8: Departure

Mid-Pacific, 12°46’N 167°12’W<br>
Research Vessel JOIDES Resolution<br>
03:37 ship time<br>

Three sharp knocks. Then three more.

Marcus surfaced from sleep like a diver coming up too fast - disoriented, his body responding before his mind caught up. The cabin was dark. The knocking had stopped.

“Marcus.” Okon’s voice, muffled through the door. Not raised, but carrying. “Elena’s found something. Lab. Now.”

Footsteps, already receding down the passageway.

Marcus sat up, blinking. The green glow of his watch read 03:38. He could hear Okon’s stride fading - not running, but purposeful, the gait of someone who had already decided this couldn’t wait.

No explanation. No elaboration. Just: Lab. Now.

He dressed in the dark, his hands moving ahead of his thoughts. Something had happened with the spheres. Something that couldn't wait until morning. Something urgent enough for Okon to come in person rather than send a message.

Wait. Rather than send a message?

The thought caught briefly. Okon had come to get him. Which meant Okon had already been contacted. Which meant Elena had called Okon first, and Okon had decided - in whatever narrow window of time had passed - to collect Marcus on his way. He was only latterly being included. Brought along.

The passageway was dim at this hour, the Resolution reduced to vibration and the smell of engine oil. His footsteps rang against metal as he followed the route Okon had already taken, alone. He found himself thinking about the samples, about the machinery of collaboration - how discoveries accelerated or stalled depending on who was involved, how quickly decisions were made.

He was thinking about being helpful. About the kind of decisive action that someone in his position was uniquely placed to take. 
The lab door was open.

Light spilled into the corridor, along with voices - Elena’s precise cadence, Okon’s lower register responding. Marcus slowed just short of the threshold. Not hiding. Just orienting. Taking in the scene before entering it.

Elena stood at the central bench, her back partly turned, gesturing at a monitor. Okon was beside her, head inclined, asking questions Marcus couldn’t quite hear. They were already deep into it.

The confusion that passed through him was faint but genuine. He had come as quickly as he could - no wasted motion, no delay - and yet here they were, already working, the briefing well underway.

They started without me.

The thought carried no accusation. It was simply observation. But beneath it sat an unasked question Marcus Holt had never learned to frame: Why would they wait?

He stepped into the light.

“Alright. Walk me through it.”

Elena showed him what had changed.

"The thermal data you saw — that was just the beginning." She pulled up the cryomagnetometer footage first: the sphere lifting from the sample stage, hanging motionless in the chamber, exhibiting a Meissner effect in material that had no right to exhibit one. Marcus felt his breath catch. This was beyond the frost on the probe. This was levitation.

But it was the spatial locking that changed everything. On the monitor, Marcus watched the footage Elena had captured. The ship rolling in heavy swell. Clay particles drifting across the field of view. And at the centre of the frame, motionless, a sphere that refused to move with anything else.

"It cut through fired ceramic," Elena said. "The dish moved. The sphere didn't."

Marcus stared at the screen. The implications were already cascading through his mind - applications, patents, the kind of discovery that remade careers. A material that maintained absolute position. A material that acted as a perfect heat sink. The potential for room-temperature quantum coherence. The phrases assembled themselves without his permission, the vocabulary of opportunity. He became aware that Okon was watching him.

“These are not geological samples,” Okon said. His voice was quiet, but something had settled behind his eyes - a weight Marcus recognised from briefings that had gone wrong, from conversations where the stakes had suddenly shifted. “They are not any kind of sample I have a framework for.” He turned to face them both.

“We are out of our depth. Every hour we continue working with equipment designed for rocks is an hour we risk damaging something we don’t understand.”

The pause that followed had a quality of decision. Marcus could feel it - the moment tilting, the trajectory being set.

“I have colleagues at the University of Hawaii,” Okon continued. “There’s a facility at Manoa - vibration-isolated, equipped for quantum-scale analysis. And I want to reach out to Caroline Vasquez - she's in NASA's astromaterials division. If these objects have the properties you’re describing…” He shook his head. “We need to know if anyone else has seen anything like this.”

Marcus heard himself speak. "Characterizing something like this — a material with properties that aren't in any literature — that's beyond what we can do shipboard. Beyond what most labs can do." He paused, the next thought arriving with the particular brightness of someone finding a way to be useful. "I have a contact at Quantiv. Rachel Kern — she's connected to their quantum materials research. They presented on coherence phenomena at AGU. They'd have the resources, the computational capacity to actually model what we're looking at.”

"Commercial." Okon's tone was polite but final. "No contractual framework, no data-sharing agreement, no clarity on IP. We'd be handing them leverage we can't afford to give.”

Marcus felt the suggestion collapse beneath him. "I just thought—"
"I've already reached out," Okon continued, and the pivot had the quality of a door closing. "Dave Palmer — he's at DeepMind now. Their materials science group, using AI to model crystal structures, characterize novel compounds. He did his graduate work in computational astrophysics, pivoted into machine learning. We published together six years ago. There's already a non-disclosure framework in place." A pause. "If anyone can help us understand what we're looking at, it's his team.”

Already reached out. Already handled. Marcus let the words pass over him. Okon was building his architecture - the careful, credentialed, academically defensible architecture. The kind that took months. The kind that let discoveries grow cold while committees deliberated. He nodded. There was nothing else to do - not here, not through these channels. But Rachel's message was still in his inbox. Quantiv didn't wait for frameworks.

Okon paused - the slight hesitation of someone aware they were about to propose rather than decide.

"I think it makes sense for Elena and me to take the Hawaii trip. The Manoa contacts are mine, and Elena's documentation has been—" He stopped, recalibrated. "Someone needs to keep the shipboard work running. Maintain the core logs, coordinate with the drilling team. I'd suggest no further analysis until we understand what we're dealing with.”

It was framed as suggestion. They both knew it was conclusion. Marcus felt the small, familiar pressure - the moment where he could assert equal standing, remind Okon that co-chief meant co-chief, that decisions of this magnitude required actual consultation rather than choreographed consensus. He had done it before, on other expeditions, with other colleagues who had mistaken his collegiality for deference.

But the words didn't come. Or rather, they started to form and then dissolved, replaced by something quieter: the knowledge that fighting for position on this ship had suddenly become less important than he'd assumed.

"That makes sense," he said. "I'll keep things steady here."
Okon held his gaze for a moment - reading, perhaps, or simply confirming. Then he nodded and turned back to the samples.
 Elena was already reaching for her notebook, her attention narrowing to the work. The conversation had ended. The decisions had been made.

Marcus remained for a few minutes longer, asking questions that were answered, offering observations that were acknowledged. Then he excused himself. 

He walked back to his cabin alone, the ship quiet around him. In his pocket, his phone held Rachel Kern’s message, still unanswered. If there’s any possibility of obtaining samples…
He didn’t take it out. Not yet. But he was aware of its weight, the way you became aware of a door you’d noticed but not yet opened.

Before long there would be helicopters, departures, the machinery of discovery accelerating beyond his reach. Tonight there was only the ship, the samples, and the long hours until morning. He lay on his bunk and stared at the ceiling and did not sleep for a long time.

The next day passed in the particular limbo of a ship waiting for something to happen. Drilling suspended. Documentation continuing. The helicopter confirmed for tomorrow morning. Marcus moved through his duties with the automatic competence of someone whose mind was elsewhere — checking manifests, coordinating with the deck crew, answering questions he barely heard. Rachel’s message sat in his inbox, unanswered, growing heavier by the hour. 

That night, somewhere past 1am, the thought surfaced through the murk of half-sleep like something that had been waiting to be noticed. She’ll still be working.

He knew it the way he knew the ship’s rhythms, the way he’d learned to read Elena’s particular brand of diligence over the weeks at sea. She wouldn’t stop. Not tonight. Not with the helicopter coming, the samples to document, the chain of custody that mattered more to her than rest or reason.

She would be alone in the lab, exhausted, making the small errors that exhaustion bred. He lay still for another twenty minutes, listening to the ship's settled rhythms, feeling the thought take shape. It wasn’t a plan. It was just… observation. Noticing that she would be there, that the samples would be there, that the lab would be empty of everyone except Elena and whatever she was documenting.

He got up. Dressed in the dark. Filled a paper cup with coffee from the mess - the pot was old, burnt, but it was something to carry. A reason to be walking the corridors at this hour. A gesture of collegial concern.

The passageways were empty. His footsteps echoed against metal. He didn’t think about where he was going or why. He just walked, coffee in hand, toward the light that would be spilling from the lab door.

It was past 2am when Marcus found Elena still at the workstation. Her face was lit by the pale glow of the microscope’s display. She was logging sphere positions, cross-referencing density readings - building the evidentiary chain that would matter when this discovery reached the wider world. The wider world that she and Okon would carry it to, while Marcus remained. Something flickered at the edge of his attention - a destination, an absence - but he was already looking back at the screen.

She didn’t hear him enter. The first she knew of his presence was a paper cup of coffee appearing at her elbow.

“You need to sleep,” Marcus said.

Elena blinked, pulling her attention from the screen. Her eyes were red-rimmed, bruised with exhaustion. She looked at him with an expression he couldn’t quite read - surprise, perhaps, at finding him here. Or something else.

“I’m almost done,” she said. “Another few hours.”

“You’ve been saying that since dinner.” He pulled up a chair, positioned it closer than strictly necessary. Close enough to see the screen. Close enough to help. “Elena. You found them. You’ve earned the right to rest. Let someone else carry this for a while.”

She shook her head, the reflex of someone who had built a career on not trusting others to finish what she’d started. “Chain of custody. I started the documentation, I need to—”

“I’m co-chief scientist.” The words came out gently, reasonably - the voice of someone offering assistance rather than asserting authority. “I have the same clearance you do. The same authority.” He paused, let the pause do its work. “And frankly, you’re exhausted. Exhausted people make mistakes. Mistakes in documentation at this stage could compromise everything we’ve found.”

It was a reasonable point, reasonably made. He watched Elena’s resistance soften, her body settling into the chair as the accumulated weight of eighteen hours pressed down on her.

“It’s not protocol,” she said, but her voice had weakened.

“Protocol is a guideline, not a suicide pact.” He smiled - warm, collegial, the smile of someone who had her best interests at heart. “I’m pulling rank, Elena. For your own good. Go. Sleep. I’ll finish the batch you’re working on and lock everything down properly. You can review it all in the morning with fresh eyes.”

Elena hesitated. He could see her thinking, weighing his offer against something she couldn’t quite articulate. Their working relationship had been cordial but distant - two professionals respecting each other’s territory, rarely overlapping. This was different. This was… something.

But she was so tired. Her eyes ached. Her thoughts kept slipping sideways, the small errors accumulating at the edge of her awareness.

“You’re sure?” she asked.

“I’m sure.” Marcus was already reaching for the keyboard, his posture shifting into the efficient focus of someone ready to work. “I’ll have everything logged by morning. Now go, before I have to physically remove you from this chair.”

Elena stood. Her body felt heavy, grateful for permission to stop.

“Thank you,” she said. “I don’t know why you’re—” She paused, unsure how to finish the sentence. Being kind? Acting human? “Thank you.”

Marcus didn’t look up from the screen. “Get some rest, Elena. Tomorrow’s going to be complicated.”

She left. The door closed behind her.

Marcus continued working for several minutes, fingers moving across the keyboard with practised efficiency. He completed Elena’s documentation batch, checking her work, noting the meticulous precision of her methodology. He reviewed the sample inventory - thousands of spheres from a single core, every entry timestamped and initialled. The evidentiary chain she’d been building, link by careful link.

He was, in every visible respect, a colleague fulfilling a promise. Then he stopped.

He sat very still, staring at the screen. The ship creaked around him, the Resolution shifting in the Pacific swell. The lab was silent except for the hum of equipment and the distant rumble of generators. He was alone with the samples and the inventory and the small, patient thought that had been growing clearer since Okon spoke those words in the briefing:

You'll coordinate the shipboard team in our absence.

That wasn't quite what Okon had said - but it was what Marcus had heard. Stay here. Keep things running. Wait.

He thought about Rachel's message, still sitting in his inbox like an unopened door. If there's any possibility of obtaining samples… He thought about Quantiv's resources, their equipment, their ability to move at speeds academic science couldn't match. He thought about the helicopter that would arrive tomorrow and the two people who would board it while he remained.

He thought about the committees that deliberated while discoveries grew cold. The months lost to bureaucracy and clearances. The way academic science moved at the glacial speed of consensus, when what it needed — what it had always needed — was someone willing to act.
*This isn’t about that*, he told himself. *This is about the science. About getting the discovery where it needs to go.*

The sample inventory glowed on the screen in front of him. And there, in the notes column, a category that existed in every scientific expedition: *Samples lost or destroyed during analysis*.

It happened. Destructive testing was part of the process. Some samples didn’t survive examination. The documentation existed precisely because loss was expected.

*This isn’t stealing*, he thought. *This is… allocation. Removing friction. Getting the science where it can actually be studied properly.*

The rationalisation felt thin, but it held. It was enough.

He opened the sample inventory. Found an entry that wouldn’t be missed - Core section 7-C, subsample 23, a batch that had already been subjected to preliminary analysis. His fingers moved across the keyboard with the steady precision of someone who had made a decision and simply needed to execute it.

*Material exhausted during thermal conductivity testing. No recoverable sample remaining.*

He saved the file.

Then he opened the sample drawer and found the vial — glass, stoppered, nearly full of grey-brown sediment. It was cool against his palm. Far heavier than it had any right to be. He slipped it into his pocket and felt it settle against his thigh, a small dense weight that would travel with him now.
The whole thing took less than two minutes. No alarms. No witnesses. Just a small administrative edit and a vial that now officially didn’t exist.

*This isn’t about me*, he thought. *This is about getting the science done right. I’m just the person willing to make it happen.*

He finished the documentation, exactly as promised. He locked down the workstation, exactly as promised. He walked back to his cabin with the vial in his pocket, small as a finger, heavy as a hammer. He placed it in his field kit, nested among the other sample containers, and closed the lid. He slept well that night. He usually did.

When Elena reviewed the logs the next morning, she found everything in order. Meticulous, even. Better than she could have managed in her exhausted state. She made a mental note to thank Marcus again, and then she heard it - distant at first, a thrum at the edge of perception that built into something impossible to ignore. The helicopter. There was no more time for thinking.

Marcus helped with the loading. Sample cases, documentation boxes, the careful transfer of materials that couldn't be jostled or dropped. He worked alongside the deck crew, efficient, one more body in the choreography of departure. One of the cases needed two people where one should have been enough. The crewman on the other handle glanced at him, eyebrows raised, but said nothing. Marcus said nothing back.

The helicopter sat on the aft deck like something from another world - bright orange against the grey steel, its rotors still, all that noise and force suspended. When the pilot began the startup sequence, Marcus stepped back with everyone else, retreating to the safe distance marked by faded paint on the deck plates.

The turbine whine built slowly at first, then climbed — a sound that bypassed the ears and entered through the chest, through the teeth, through the bones of the skull. The rotors began to turn, lazy at first, then faster, then blurring into a disc that chopped the air into something unbreathable. Wind tore across the deck in horizontal gusts, finding gaps in clothing, pressing tears from eyes, turning the simple act of standing into negotiation.

Marcus watched through slitted eyes as Elena climbed aboard. Then Okon, stooping beneath the rotor wash, one hand clamped on his hard hat. The sample cases were already secured inside — days of documentation, thousands of spheres, the evidence of something unprecedented.

The door slid shut.
The engine note changed. The machine grew light on its skids, swaying slightly, and then it lifted - not gracefully, not gently, but with the brutal assertion of physics overcome by force. It rose through its own chaos of downdraft, climbing above the derrick, above the ship’s superstructure, above the reach of anything that might hold it back.

Marcus stood on the deck with the wind still battering his face and watched the helicopter grow smaller. The noise faded from assault to pressure to distant thunder to nothing. The orange speck tracked across the sky, steady and purposeful, carrying the discovery toward facilities and expertise and the machinery of proper science.

He watched until it disappeared.
The deck crew dispersed around him, returning to their duties, the interruption already folding into routine. The ship reasserted itself - the familiar rumble of generators, the creak of the drill string, the patient work of a vessel that had no opinion about who came or went.

Marcus remained at the rail for a long moment, looking at the empty sky where the helicopter had been.

Then he went below to send an email.
The vial was still in his cabin, nested in his field kit among the other sample containers. Small. Glass. Officially nonexistent.

He had work to do.
