# Chapter 2: Beijing

Beijing, Haidian District<br>
06:47 local time<br>

The blood came with the first brush stroke, as it always did.

Liang Chen watched it pool in the foam, pink against white, and felt the familiar irritation - not at the pain, which was negligible, but at the persistence. Twenty years of bleeding gums. Twenty years of dentists suggesting softer bristles, gentler pressure, better floss. He’d tried all of it. The bleeding continued, obedient to its own logic.

He spat, rinsed, spat again. In the mirror, a fifty-two-year-old man looked back at him: thinning hair cropped short, the slight pouching beneath the eyes that no amount of sleep seemed to cure, a face that had settled into an expression of permanent mild concentration. Not handsome, not ugly. The kind of face that witnesses remembered poorly, which had served him well enough in his profession.

Liu Mei was already in the kitchen. He could hear the electric kettle reaching its pitch, the clink of her spoon against porcelain. They would eat breakfast in near-silence, as they had for years, the television providing what conversation could not. This morning it was a segment on high-speed rail expansion - the new Chengdu line, completion dates, economic projections. Facts delivered in the bright, frictionless tone of state media. He listened without hearing, chewing his congee, watching his wife scroll through her phone.

She worked in the taxation bureau. Numbers, compliance, the orderly processing of obligations. They had met at a work-unit social event in 1998, married in 2000, produced a son in 2004. The mathematics of a life. Sometimes Chen wondered if she had chosen him or simply not refused him - whether their marriage was a decision or merely the path of least resistance, two people drifting into proximity and staying there because the effort of separation exceeded the discomfort of remaining.

He did not ask. Some questions, once voiced, could not be unvoiced.

“Jianwen called,” Liu Mei said, not looking up from her phone.

Their son. Chen felt the small contraction in his chest that always accompanied news of the boy. “When?”

“Last night. You were asleep. He needs his hukou registration for something at the university.”

“I’ll find it.”

“It’s in the grey folder. Second drawer.”

Of course she knew where it was. Liu Mei knew where everything was. The apartment was her domain, organised according to principles he had long stopped trying to understand. He existed within it like a tenant in a well-managed property, broadly content, dimly aware that the true order of things was beyond his comprehension.

He finished his congee, washed the bowl, dried his hands on the cloth that hung from the cabinet handle. The cloth was damp; it was always damp. In eighteen years he had never known it to be fully dry. A small mystery he had stopped investigating.

“I’ll be late tonight,” he said. “A report.”

She nodded without looking up. He was often late. The reports were always urgent until they weren’t, then new reports took their place. The work never ended, merely transformed. She had stopped asking about it years ago - partly discretion, partly disinterest, partly the understanding that his answers would be vague regardless of her questions.

He took the bus.

The 375 was half-empty at this hour, the rush not yet peaked. Chen found his usual spot near the back door, close enough to exit quickly, far enough from the driver to avoid the blast of cold air when the doors opened. He had calculated this position years ago, refined it through repetition. Small efficiencies. The texture of a life.

Outside, Haidian scrolled past: construction hoardings promising luxury developments, the grey-glass towers of tech companies, street vendors setting up breakfast stalls, the endless flow of e-bikes weaving through traffic. The Zhongguancun corridor, China’s Silicon Valley, where fortunes were made by people half his age with ideas he could not evaluate and confidence he could not fathom.

Chen’s relationship to technology was instrumental. He understood systems - how data moved, where it pooled, the chokepoints where observation was possible. But the enthusiasm that drove the young engineers who built these towers, the quasi-religious fervour for disruption and scale, remained foreign to him. He had joined the apparatus in 1995 because it offered stability, structure, a clear hierarchy of responsibilities. The work had changed around him - paper files giving way to databases, databases to algorithmic triage, human judgment increasingly subordinated to pattern-matching systems he didn’t fully understand - but his function remained constant. He was a filter. Information flowed through him, and he decided what mattered.

Or rather, he decided what to *report* as mattering. The distinction had once seemed trivial. Now, three decades in, he understood it was everything.

The bus stopped at Weigongcun, and a young woman boarded with a child, perhaps three years old, bundled in a puffy red jacket. The child stared at Chen with the unnerving directness of the very young - no social mask, no performed disinterest. He looked away, unsettled. Children saw too clearly. They hadn’t yet learned what not to notice.

The child’s mother found seats near the front, and Chen returned his gaze to the window. His reflection floated ghost-like over the passing street, transparent, insubstantial. He thought, not for the first time, that this was how he moved through the world - present but not quite solid, a figure others looked through rather than at.

The bus turned onto Zhongguancun South Street, and the MSS compound came into view, set back from the road behind its grey perimeter wall. Unremarkable. Deliberately so. The building itself was a squat concrete block, indistinguishable from a hundred other government facilities - the architecture of bureaucratic anonymity. No signage announced its function. No guards were visible from the street. The security was in the systems, the protocols, the layers of clearance that determined who saw what and when.

Chen disembarked at his usual stop and walked the remaining three hundred metres, his breath misting in the cold air. January in Beijing: the sky a pale grey, the pollution not yet lifted, the city hunched against the chill. He showed his credentials at the unmarked entrance, passed through the scanner, deposited his phone in the secure locker where personal devices were stored during work hours. The familiar rituals of entry. The shedding of his civilian self.

His office was on the fourth floor, a space he shared with two colleagues: Wang, who was meticulous and dull, and Feng, who was young and eager in ways that Chen found exhausting. Their desks formed a rough triangle around a central filing cabinet, a geometry of uneasy coexistence. The room smelled of instant coffee and the particular staleness of air that had been filtered and recirculated too many times.

Wang was already at his desk, scrolling through something with the glazed expression of a man who had long ago stopped seeing what he looked at. Feng’s chair was empty - he would arrive in twenty minutes, apologising for the metro, carrying a paper cup of tea from the stand outside, radiating the anxious energy of someone who believed his career still had a trajectory.

Chen sat, logged in, and began the morning’s triage. 
The work was mostly noise.

This was the thing civilians didn’t understand about intelligence: the overwhelming majority of collected data was meaningless. Intercepted communications, flagged transactions, algorithmic alerts — the systems gathered everything, and human analysts sorted the chaff from the wheat. Except there was almost never any wheat. There were only gradations of chaff, degrees of irrelevance, the endless sifting through digital detritus for patterns that almost never resolved into significance.

Chen had learned to read quickly, to skim for anomaly, to let his attention slide over the predictable and catch on the unexpected. This morning’s queue held the usual mix: academic conference registrations, export licence applications, flagged emails from universities with defence research programmes. Most would be filed and forgotten. A few would be marked for secondary review. Perhaps one in a hundred would generate a report that travelled upward to someone who might act on it.

He worked steadily through the morning, pausing twice for tea from the communal kettle, once to use the bathroom where the hand dryer had been broken for three months and no one had reported it. At 11:40 he walked to the canteen, ate a bowl of tomato and egg over rice, returned to his desk by 12:15. The afternoon stretched ahead, identical to the morning.

Outside his window, the grey Beijing afternoon slowly progressed into the darkening of the evening. The pollution had thickened; the towers of Zhongguancun had become vague shapes, shadows against a sky the colour of ash. Chen worked until 18:30, logged out, retrieved his phone from the secure locker, and walked to the bus stop.

The noodle shop was three blocks from the bus stop nearest his apartment, down a narrow street that smelled of frying oil and cigarette smoke. The owner, a thin Gansu man with nicotine-stained fingers, nodded as Chen entered. No words were exchanged. The order was understood: beef noodles, no coriander, extra chilli oil.

Chen took his usual table by the window, facing the door. The restaurant was nearly empty — it was early yet, the dinner rush still an hour away. He ate slowly, scrolling through news on his phone. A corruption trial in Guangdong. A tech billionaire’s philanthropic initiative. The American president saying something inflammatory; the Foreign Ministry’s measured response.

The noodles were good. They were always good. This consistency was why he came - the reliability, the absence of surprise. His life was structured around such small anchors: the bus route, the desk, the evening bowl of noodles. They made the days navigable. They imposed order on time that might otherwise blur into formlessness.

He finished, paid in cash, nodded to the owner, and walked home.

The apartment was dark when he entered. Liu Mei was at a work function - She’d mentioned it this morning, or perhaps yesterday; he couldn’t remember. He turned on the lights, removed his shoes, padded to the kitchen in his socks. The television came on automatically, tuned to a documentary about the Silk Road. He let it play without watching.

In the refrigerator he found leftover bok choy and a container of rice. He heated them in the microwave, ate standing at the counter, washed the dishes immediately. The routine was important. Without it, the evenings expanded into shapeless hours, time pooling around him like water.

At 21:00 he sat at the small desk in the bedroom’s corner and opened his laptop. The cursor blinked in the text field of the electronics forum, waiting.

He had discovered this outlet three years ago, almost by accident - a review of a router he’d purchased, written in a moment of frustration when the device failed to perform as advertised. The act of writing plainly, of saying exactly what he thought without diplomatic hedging or institutional circumlocution, had been unexpectedly satisfying. He’d written another review. Then another. Now he had 847 posts across various consumer categories: routers, keyboards, humidifiers, electric kettles, USB cables.

No one knew about this. Not Liu Mei, who would find it beneath him. Not his colleagues, who would find it bizarre. The anonymity was the point - not protection from consequences, but freedom from identity. On the forum he was not Liang Chen, senior intelligence analyst. He was user4729, a man with opinions about build quality and customer service response times.

Tonight he wrote 300 words about a Xiaomi air purifier that had developed a rattle after four months. The review was precise, detailed, mildly damning. He rated the product three stars out of five and recommended the Philips alternative. Someone would read this. Someone would perhaps make a different purchasing decision because of it. A tiny intervention in the world, but *his*.

He posted the review, closed the laptop, and prepared for bed.

At 22:43, the chair scraped.

He was lying in darkness, not yet asleep, when the sound came through the ceiling - the familiar grinding drag of furniture across concrete. His neighbour, performing whatever nightly ritual required this adjustment. Every night. Every single night. The same sound, the same time, give or take ten minutes.

Chen lay still, listening to his own heartbeat, feeling the anger rise and recognising its absurdity. This was nothing. A sound. A neighbour with a chair. The most trivial of irritations. And yet it enraged him in a way he couldn’t fully explain - not the noise itself, but the helplessness it represented. The inability to control even this small aspect of his environment. The way other people’s lives pressed in on his own, demanding accommodation, refusing to be silent.

The scraping stopped. Silence returned, thick and incomplete, underlaid by the faint hum in his right ear that never quite went away.

Chen closed his eyes and waited for sleep, listening to the silence where the chair had been, the negative space of a sound he knew would return tomorrow, and the night after, and every night until something finally changed.
